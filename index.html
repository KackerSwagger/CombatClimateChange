Intro

One of the biggest challenges facing humanity comes from how we interact with our environment. Society, as it stands today, does not have a sustainable future with our ever increasing pressure on Earth’s resources and altercation of the environment. Climate change is among the most conerning issues and this site aims to break down the facts, address misconceptions, and explore the highest impact solutions.

The Scale of Our Emissions

With the impending climate crisis, it is important to investigate which nationstates and economic sectors emit the most GHGs (greenhouse gases). By being able to pinpoint where most pollution originates from, we can prioritize which sources to address and create a strategy that could fully encompass the multifaceted nature of the problem. There are many different sources of GHGs and it is important to identify which ones are the most polluting so that we could use our time and resources effectively to prevent a rapid change in our global temperatures.

There are many different scales that could be used to identify GHGs emission sources. On the larger end of the scale, we have emissions by nationstates. There are two measurements that are useful, which are the total emissions per year, and the historical emissions the country has emitted since fossil fuels have been utilized. At this scale, it is useful in identifying which nations bear responsibility in reducing GHG emissions, but this is a tricky discussion beyond the scope of this article (see geopolitical solutions for further discussion).

On another scale are emissions by the economic sector or the economic activities that lead to the production of GHGs. There is variation by how the economic sectors are categorized and which polluting processes are included, but generally the major sectors are: Electricity, Industry, agriculture, land-use change and forestry, buildings, transportation, and waste.

Global greenhouse gas emissions by sector gives us a holistic view as to where the major sources are found within our international economy. For policymakers however, it would be more useful to work with emissions by sector within the nation. Due to specialization within the global economy, some nations may find their emissions to be dominated by the industry sector like Indonesia whereas other countries like the USA have the majority of their emissions coming from transport (see Countries of interest for a profile on each nation’s emissions). The production of emissions in most nations however comes from the electricity and heat production sector specifically from burning fossil fuels. Among the highest emitting nations, a common trend is that emissions mostly stem from electricity and heat production as the demand of energy is high, either for the general population’s lifestyle or for manufacturing purposes. The energy demand is met through the burning of fossil fuels but with coal being especially prevalent in China and India (see Countries of Interest). coal releases the most CO2 per thermal unit of energy when burned compared to other fossil fuels.

More recently, the sources of GHGs can be viewed from individual companies rather than nationally. Based on the carbon majors report, 100 companies accounted for 71% of global cumulative emissions from 1988-2015. Of the 100 companies included, 41 public investor-owned companies, 16 private investor-owned companies, 36 state-owned companies, and 7 state producers. It is important to note that the study used both scope 1 and scope 3 emissions in its calculation with scope 3 emissions accounting for 90% of a company’s total emissions. This is an important note because a larger portion of scope 3 emissions can come from the consumer. Scope 1 emissions are generally emissions that are produced onsite and from the organization's vehicles. Scope 3 emissions can be broken down further into two categories, upstream and downstream emissions. Upstream processes occur before the production of the good and service and can include emissions from material acquisition and preprocessing. Downstream occurs after the good is produced, and processes usually account for the majority of scope 3 emissions. Its sources can include emissions from the distribution and storage of goods, consumer use (like gasoline in a car), and end-of-life disposal. See more on emission scopes here

GHG emissions from nations, economic sectors, companies, and individuals are all various scales that could be used to determine where the majority of emissions originate from. Consequently, knowing the origin can help assign responsibility on reducing these emissions as well as giving us strategic points to attack for maximum impact. For example, understanding that a large proportion of GHGs comes from the burning of coal in many of the top polluting countries should give us a major incentive to decommission coal-fired power plants and use alternatives that are ideally renewable. While it may seem as though individuals have a minimal impact on emissions, it is individuals that make up our world and therefore it is our responsibility to preserve it. As individuals, we’ve formed collectives in the face of danger and oppression and we must all do our part to preserve this planet. (see personal action on what you could do).
